Musical terms

text setting

  • syllabic: one note per syllable of text
  • melismatic: many notes per syllable of text

  • formes fixes: "fixed forms"; for the same text you use the same music
  • refrain: the same text (recurring musical passages found) you find in structures like formes fixes
  • ritornello: recurring instrumental passage between stanza of texts

  • word-painting: music must match the meaning of the words
  • recitative: (more everyday speech like) natural word-rhythm; light accompaniment by basso continuo (not too loud to cover the voice of the singer = bass line + chords)
  • aria: (less speech-like, to express emotions) rhythms and repetitions “imposed” on the words, without violating their normal accentuation; sounds more musically organized; repetitions of both text and melody possible; fuller instrumental accompaniment than just the basso continuo; usually has a ritornello in Monteverdi

composing

  • cantus firmus: the “fixed song”; what the composer takes from earlier music and uses as the basis for a new piece
  • tenor: the lowest “held” voice in an organum; it was originally a Gregorian chant, now used as a cantus firmus; this meaning is different from tenor as a male vocal register

Chants

Monophony

Monophony is a musical texture consisting of a single melodic line. It is the oldest type of music, and is still used today. Gregorian chant is an example of monophony.

  • Kyrie XI
    • Gregorian chant
    • written by monks whose names do not survive
    • "kyrie"s are different in each repetition but "elesion" is the same
  • O Viridissima Virga
    • plainchant
    • Hildegard of Bingen (the earliest named composer in the western tradition)
    • in honor of the Virgin Mary
    • drone: an instrument with only a single pitch; with a drone it’s easier to recognize overlapping pitches (give you more of a sense of ambience)
  • Lanquan li jorn
    • troubadour song
    • Jaufré Rudel
    • sung poetry (courtly love); poems in stanzas and the music is strophic: the melody repeats for each stanza
    • accompanied by instrument because he's traveling and performing to people
  • Douce dame jolie
    • troubadour song; monophonic chanson
    • Guillaume de Machaut
    • formes fixes (fixed forms) of medieval French poetry: for the same text you use the same music

Polyphony

Polyphony is a musical texture consisting of two or more simultaneous lines of independent melody. Polyphony is the most common texture in Western music. It is also called counterpoint.

  • Alleluia nativitas
    • Perotin
    • organum: a type of polyphony that is based on plainchant
    • melisma: many notes to a single syllable of textt; used for emphasis or ornamentation; the genre of organum entirely melismatic because no new text is added
    • cantus firmus "fixed-song": a pre-existing melody used as the basis for a polyphonic song
    • tenor (the lowest “held” voice in an organum; it was originally a Gregorian chant, now used as a cantus firmus; this meaning is different from tenor as a male vocal register)
  • Ave Maria
    • Josquin des Prez
    • motet: composers add words to the melodies in the upper voices. This creates the genre of the motet; different from organium because it has new text
    • imitative polyphony: the same melody is repeated in different voices

Opera

  • As Vesta was from Latmos Hill descending
    • Thomas Weelkes
    • madrigal: secular polyphony beginning in later 16th century for social music-making with strong emphasis on representing the meaning of the words, featuring the theme of love, etc
    • word painting: the music reflects the meaning of the words
  • L’Orfeo
    • Claudio Monteverdi
    • recitative: a style of singing that is a halfway point between speaking and singing
    • aria: a song for solo voice with orchestral accompaniment
    • ritornello: a recurring instrumental passage
  • L’amante segretto
    • Strozzi
    • cantata: multi-sectioned solo pieces; vocal piece with instrumental accompaniment; contains recitative and aria or more organized sections
    • ostinato: repeating pattern of notes, usually played by instruments in the bass register, like the four-descending-note pattern in the Strozzi -> compassion
