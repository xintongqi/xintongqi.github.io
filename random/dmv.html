4 Traffic control

Directions given by traffic officers take precedence over everything else.

Traffic signs

  • Regulation signs (rectangle, red)

Stop sign (full stop), yield sign (decrease speed prepare to stop), speed limit, no right turn, do not pass…

  • Warnings signs (diamond, yellow)

People crossing, narrow bridge, road hazard

  • Work area signs (diamond, orange): road work sign…must reduce speed even w/o speed limit
  • Destination signs (green): show where to go and distance to locations
  • Route signs (white): indicate what route this is, e.g. route 60. State, interstate and US signs are different
  • Service signs (blue): gas station nearby

Traffic Lights

If the traffic light is not working, you should treat it as a STOP sign.

  • Steady RED: Stop.

Red Arrow: Stop; no turn in the arrow’s direction.

Flashing Red: Stop → treat as STOP sign.

Red + Green Arrow: You may go only in the arrow’s direction; others must stop.

  • Steady YELLOW: Light is about to turn red → prepare to stop.

Flashing Yellow: Proceed with caution.

  • Steady GREEN: Go if clear; must yield to pedestrians and vehicles still in the intersection.

Lane Control Signals (on overhead lanes)

Red X: Lane closed; do not use this lane.

Steady Yellow X: Lane closing soon, move out safely.

Flashing Yellow X: Shared center lane for left turn only.

Green Arrow: Open lane, you can use it.

Pavement Markings

Center Lines

  • Double solid yellow: No passing either direction, unless you’re entering or existing a driveway.
  • Single solid yellow: Can pass or change lanes, but only do so when necessary.
  • Solid yellow + broken yellow: Passing allowed only on the broken side.
  • Broken yellow: Passing allowed.
  • White lines: less strict than yellow lines

Arrows

Only go in direction(s) the arrows show.

Stop line & crosswalk

You see these lines when you’re around a STOP sign, YIELD sign or traffic light.

If it’s one single line, it’s called a stop line. Otherwise called a crosswalk.

Stop before the line or crosswalk.

Reserved lanes

Marked with a diamond (♦) → reserved HOV (high occupancy vehicles)/bus/bike lanes.

Cannot enter or use these lanes unless you are driving those vehicles.

If the white lane is broken, you can enter if you’re making a right turn at the next intersection.

Center turn lane

Two-way left turn lane → enter only to turn left, not to pass.

5 Intersections and Turns

Right-of-Way Rules

  • Yield to pedestrians in crosswalks at all times.
  • At an intersection, when making a left turn, yield to oncoming traffic going straight or turning right. But if you arrived first at the intersection, they should yield to you.
  • Emergency vehicles: always yield and pull over.
  • Stop when you enter a roadway from a driveway, private road, etc.

Move Over Laws

When police/fire/ambulance/tow trucks/highway maintenance vehicles approach with flashing light, you must either move over to the adjacent lane or slow down if you cannot move over.

Turns

Always signal before turning or changing lanes (at least 100f/30m ahead).

If you turn left, left turn signal lamps should flash; if you turn right, right turn signals should flash. If you stop, put your arm down.

If you turn signals aren’t working, signal with your arms (point left means left turn, point up means right turn)

U-turns (going to the opposite direction)

Usually not allowed on highways, near curves or hills, or where other drivers cannot see you from 500f/150m away in either direction.

U-turns are not allowed in business districts/school zones of NYC and where No U-turn signs are provided.

6 Passing

When you pass, give the vehicle the full lane width. Never move into the same lane even if the lane is wide and the vehicle is on one side.

When you’re being passed, decrease your speed slightly and stay centered in your lane.

You cannot pass if

  • The lane has a solid yellow center line.
  • On curves or hills where you can’t see ahead
  • Within 100f/30m of:
  • Intersections
  • Railroad crossings
  • Bridges, tunnels, or underpasses
  • When approaching or stopped for:
  • A school bus with red lights. You must stop even if the bus is on the opposite side of a divided highway.
  • Emergency vehicles with lights/sirens
  • Never pass a vehicle that has stopped to let the pedestrian pass.

You are usually only allowed to pass on the left side of the vehicle. You can pass on the right if:

  • The vehicle ahead is turning left, or
  • You are on a road with two or more lanes going in the same direction.

How to pass:

  1. Use turn signal lamps and arms signals at least 100f/30m before you make a lane change.
  2. Do not exceed speed limits when you pass.
  3. After you pass you should return to your lane when you see the front bumper of the other car in your mirror.
  4. Signal again when you finish passing. (e.g. pass on left, first signal left, then signal right)

7 Parallel Parking

When we say parking, we mean three things: parking, standing, stopping

  • No parking: driver cannot move, but passengers and goods OK
  • No standing: driver cannot move, no goods, but passengers OK

No parking or standing (but stopping allowed) if:

  • Within 20f/6m of fire station driveway
  • Within 30f/10m of traffic light, STOP sign or YIELD sign
  • Within 20f/6m of a crosswalk at an intersection
  • Within 50f/15m of railroad crossing
  • No stopping: nothing stopping at all for any purpose unless it’s to obey traffic signs, signals, etc. No stopping if:
  • Within 15f/5m of a fire hydrant
  • Within 30f/10m of pedestrian safety area
  • Double parking: you park next to a parked car on the driveway
  • In an intersection, on a sidewalk or a crosswalk, on railroad tracks, on a bridge or in a tunnel

Some parking is reserved for people with disabilities. Do not park, stand, stop without permission. If the person who receives the permit is not in the vehicle, you cannot park either.

How to park:

  • Step 1,2:
  • Stop 2–3 feet from the parked car ahead of the space
  • Signal other drivers: if you are parking to the right signal right otherwise left
  • Step 3: if you’re parking to the right, turn wheels fully right
  • Step 4: When your front clears the rear car, turn wheels fully left
  • Step 5:
  • Straighten wheels, center the car
  • Your wheels must be no more than 1ft from road curb
  • Step 6: if you park on a hill, set your parking brakes

How to pull out

  • Turn your head to look, use interior rearview mirror
  • Signal other drivers, check side mirrors
  • Turn your head to look over your left shoulder, drive into traffic

8 Defensive Driving

Speed: without specific limit, drive under 55mph(88km/h). In NYC, drive under 25mph(40km/h)

Vehicle condition

Brakes

  • Car pulling to one side when braking → problem
  • Wet brakes can be dried by lightly pressing the pedal. If that doesn’t work, check immediately

Steering

  • Steering wheel have excessive free movement → problem
  • Whining noise during sharp turns → problem
  • If your vehicle has power steering must maintain the fluid level (usually old cars)

Lights

  • Keep headlights and lenses clean and unbroken
  • Cracked lenses cause glare to other drivers
  • Headlights must be properly aimed

Tires

  • Legal minimum tread depth: 2/32 inch/0.16 cm
  • Wear bars show when tires must be replaced
  • Penny test: if you see all of Lincoln’s head, tires are too worn
  • Tires with cuts, bulges, or exposed cords are illegal
  • Maintain correct tire pressure

Glass

  • Windshields and windows must be clean and clear.
  • Replace worn wiper blades
  • Defroster, rear defogger, and washer fluid must be in good condition

Horn

  • Horn must function properly
  • Use only to warn others of danger, not to express anger

Driver condition

Be prepared and look ahead

  • Sit vertical dont slouch
  • Always look ahead and look at sides of the road
  • Do not assume other drivers will follow STOP signs and YIELD signs

Avoid driver distractions

  • Do not use your phone → calls only allowed for emergency otherwise you’ll be fined
  • Avoid arguments with passengers
  • Avoid eating
  • Make sure children and pets are restrained

Drowsy and fatigue driving → most common for young people lacking sleep

  • If you are tired, or cannot focus, do not drive; pull off and nap if necessary
  • Do not depend on radio or opening window to keep you awake
  • If you drive long trips, better have a companion
  • Take breaks every 200 miles (300km) or 2h
  • Do not drive for at least 15 min after you wake up

Aggressive drivers

Speeding → breaking traffic safety law

  • To prevent road rage, better not to make eye contact with other drivers
  • If an aggressive driver confronts you
  • Remain calm try to move away safely → be prepared to yield right of way
  • Report him with a vehicle description
  • If that driver follows you do not stop drive to the nearest police station
  • If you tend to be an aggressive driver
  • Don't panic if you are late
  • Do not drive when you are angry upset or very tired

Road rage: hostile driving → breaking criminal law

Allow yourself space

Use the two-second rule; pick an object, you and the car before you (or the car after you) should have at least two seconds apart passing that object.

If a driver follows you too closely, move to another lane, or reduce speed and pull off the road and let them go by. Do not press your brakes suddenly.

Seat belts, child safety belts and air bags

Everyone MUST wear a seat belt: driver, passenger, child, etc, except for emergency vehicles, buses, and some other vehicles.

You should sit at least 10 inches away from the air bag. Never put an infant in a rear-facing seat in the front seat of a vehicle that has a passenger air bag.

Drive safely in work zones

Orange signs, diamond shape.

Sometimes there is a flagger (a person to direct you); they are the same as signs.

Rear-end collisions are the most common type of crash in work zones.

Work zones are not stationary - changes can happen, pay attention.

Decrease your speed, keep a braking distance with you and the vehicle in front of you.

Drive through a roundabout

When you reach a roundabout, slow down, pay attention to which exit to take, yield the right of way to any pedestrians and bicyclists.

While inside the roundabout, remain in your lane until you exit. If you need to move from the inside lane to the outside lane, flash your right turn signal.

9 Alcohol and Other Drugs

Alcohol & drugs impair driving: slow reaction time, reduce judgment, blur vision, and affect coordination, even without feeling intoxicated; particularly dangerous at night

You’re legally responsible for driving impaired, even if others encouraged you or you “felt fine”. Just don’t drive - designated driver, rideshare, public transit, etc.

Alcohol

BAC (Blood Alcohol Content) → breathalyzer test (+ chemical test)

BAC only depends on how much alcohol you drink, how much time passes, and your weight. Physical fitness and food in your stomach don’t affect BAC, but may affect how you feel.

0.08% = intoxicated (DWI) → can be arrested

0.05% = ability impaired (DWAI)

0.02% = violation for drivers under 21 (Zero Tolerance Law)

Commercial drivers: lower limits apply

The only safe BAC is 0.00%. Time is the only thing that helps lower your BAC, and it takes about one hour to reduce BAC by 0.02%, which is about a can of beer.

When you illegally use your driver’s license to purchase alcoholic beverages, your license can also be suspended.

Open Container Law: if a driver or a passenger has an open container of alcoholic beverage, it counts as a traffic infraction. The same applies to drugs.

Drugs (illegal, prescription, OTC) → chemical test

Don’t do marijuana or cannabis before driving.

No matter if it's prescribed or OTC, you must read the labels and make sure it’s safe to drive.

Drugs can impair driving alone or worse when mixed with alcohol.

Penalties

  • Arrest, fines, license suspension/revocation, jail, higher insurance, permanent record
  • Refusing a breath test has automatic penalties
  • Ignition interlock program: anyone convicted of DWI will be required to install an ignition interlock device (blow into the device car will not start if alcohol detected)
  • Leandra’s Law: if the driver commits alcohol related offence with a child under 16 in the car, they will be charged with felony
  • If the driver commits alcohol related offence on a conditional license → felony

10 Special Driving Conditions

Railroad crossing

Yellow with RR and X symbol.

Some have flashing red lights and gates, some don’t.

As you approach a crossing, always expect a train.

Never race a train, never drive around lowering or rising gates, never stop on tracks.

Stop at least 15 feet/5 m before the tracks. Do not pass unless the lights have stopped flashing.

Never cross the railroad tracks unless you’re sure you’ll pass all of the tracks.

School buses (even w/o passengers), other buses, vehicles with explosive or flammable cargo must stop at all railroad crossings. If you follow them, be expected to stop.

If you stall on the tracks

Get EVERYONE out of the car and get away from the tracks.

Run toward the direction the train is coming from. If the train is approaching, run away from the tracks at a 45 degree angle to avoid the debris when the train hits your car.

Locate the emergency notification system sign and call the number.

Expressway driving

The speed limit is usually 55 mph (88 km/h) but can be 65 mph (105 km/h) in rural areas

Entering an Expressway (Merging)

  • Use the entrance ramp to speed up and reach expressway speed; wait for a large gap in traffic then speed up quickly to join the expressway
  • Yield to traffic already on the expressway → ramp traffic never has right of way
  • Signal early, merge smoothly → no stopping on the ramp unless unavoidable
  • If you enter/exit at the wrong entrance/exit, stay on the expressway until the next one

Driving on the expressway

  • Right lane: slower traffic, entering/exiting
  • Left lane: passing only → Stay out of the left lane if you are not passing
  • Do not weave between lanes
  • Always look left–center–right and check blind spots; be prepared for a merging car

 Exiting an Expressway

  • Signal before entering the deceleration lane at least 100f/30m
  • Slow down only after you are off the main roadway. Do NOT slow down on the expressway itself
  • Watch exit speed signs: ramps are sharper than they look

Stopping / Emergencies

  • Do not stop on the expressway unless it’s an emergency
  • If you must stop: pull onto the shoulder, turn on hazard lights, stay in the car if it’s unsafe to exit

Night driving

The law requires you to use your headlights 1.5h after the sunset and 1.5h before sunrise. You should also turn your headlights on at dawn, dusk or in fog, rain, snow, sleet.

Has to be headlights - daytime running lights, parking lights, won’t suffice.

Even if headlights don’t make you see better, they help other drivers and pedestrians see you.

Use low beams within 500f/150m of oncoming traffic, or 200f/60m of a vehicle ahead of you, even if the vehicle is in a different lane.

Use high beams otherwise.

Drivers also use flashing high beams to signal they can’t see your headlights clearly.

To reduce glare from lights of following vehicles, switch your inside rearview mirror to “night” position.

If you’re blinded by headlights of an approaching vehicle, slow down, look toward your right.

Driving in rain, fog, or snow, and driving in winter

During ice or snow, do not drive unless you must. If you drive, first clear the ice and snow.

Typically bridges, ramps and overpasses freeze first.

Use low beams for headlights.

Hydroplaning: happens when tires ride on water → skidding

If it occurs: let your steering wheel turn and slowly get control; press your brakes gently

Driving emergencies

  • Tire blowout/loss of a wheel: thumping sound is a warning sign

Hold the steering wheel tightly and slowly take your foot off the gas pedal. Do not use your brakes until your vehicle is controlled.

Leave the road when it’s safe to do so.

  • Steering failure

Slowly take your foot off the gas pedal and keep your foot off the brake pedal while it’s safe → you don’t want a sudden change in speed, that’s worse than going straight

  • Brake failure

If your brake goes to the floor, try pumping it. If that doesn’t work, use your emergency or parking brake, but only apply it gently.

  • Stuck gas pedal

Hook your shoe under the pedal to see if you can free it. If not, use the brake to slow your vehicle. Do not turn off the ignition as you could lose control of the steering wheel.

  • Headlight failure → try other lights
  • Blocked vision → use side window. Turn on emergency lights and pull over.
  • Running off the pavement

Do not suddenly steer. Ease your foot off the gas pedal and gently press the brake.

  • Vehicle approaching head-on in your lane

Decrease speed, pull over to the right and sound your horn.

  • Water:

If the car is still floating, do not open the door, use the window to escape.

Otherwise, go to the back seat because the front of the vehicle is heavier than the rear, then try to escape through the window.

When you rise air pressure will build in your lungs, let it out in small breaths. Do not hold your breath tight or try to blow out.

  • Fire: if you see smoke, pull off, turn off ignition and get away from the car. Call the fire department.
  • Deer: deer appear from October to December, watch out when you drive

11 Sharing the Road

Pedestrians and skateboarders

Do not only rely on mirrors when children are near. Get outside the car and check.

Always yield to pedestrians in crosswalks (marked or unmarked), even if traffic signals or other right-of-way rules are not in their favor.

Blind people can be identified by canes and guiding dogs - they have right-of-way.

If there is no sidewalk, pedestrians must walk on the side facing the oncoming traffic.

Bicyclists and in-line skaters

Bicycles and skaters are vehicles → same rights and rules, with these in addition:

  • They must ride in a bicycle line if there is one, or remain close to the curb on the right side of the road
  • Come to a full stop before they enter a roadway from a driveway, an alley or over a curb
  • Never travel with more than two side by side in a single lane
  • Never ride on sidewalk if it’s prohibited by local laws
  • They need to signal with their arm (up arm: right turn, left arm: left turn, down arm: stop)
  • Never carry infants under one year old

Never carry passengers if there’s no passenger seat

  • Keep at least one hand on the handlebars at all times
  • If they drive on a highway, need to have a horn that can be heard 100f/30m away

If they drive at night, need to have headlights that can be seen 500f/150m ahead and 300f/90m from behind

Give them enough space because they can be sent off balance by air pressure from a vehicle. Also, they can change their speed etc suddenly so watch out.

Motorcycles, moped vehicles, scooters, etc

Motorcycles are also vehicles → same rights and rules.

Harder to see; check blind spots.

Give full lane width when passing.

Motorcycles may change speed/position quickly to avoid hazards.

Large Vehicles (Buses & Trucks)

Why special attention to large vehicles? Because they bigger they are

  • The bigger the blind spot → avoid these no zones

When the truck is backing up, DO NOT pass. Just wait. Many blind spots for the driver.

  • The longer it takes for them to stop

Leave plenty of space between your car and the truck.

If you’re in front of a truck, indicate your intention to turn or change lanes early.

  • The more room they need to maneuver

Do not cut abruptly in front of a large vehicle.

Pay close attention to the signals.

Do not delay near a large vehicle.

  • The longer it takes for an auto to pass them

Make sure you can see the whole front of the vehicle before you return to your lane after you pass. Especially when you’re on a hill, trucks may speed up going downhill.

Slow-Moving & Special Vehicles

Vehicles with a reflective orange triangle symbol = slow-moving, such as animal drawn

all-terrain vehicle and snowmobiles

Horseback riders

12 Traffic Crash

Carry an emergency kit in your vehicle that has flares and first aid supplies.

At the scene

If you’re involved, stop immediately. Do not leave regardless of level of damage.

  • If it’s another car

Exchange: name, address, driver license number, vehicle registration, insurance (policy number and effective date) → give this info to the other driver and the police

  • If it’s a parked car or an animal → try to find the owner and notify the police
  • If any person is injured or killed

Do not move the injured people unless absolutely necessary.

Notify the policy immediately, call an ambulance, etc.

If you’re not involved,  leave the scene.

DMV Crash Report

You must file MV-104 with DMV within 10 days if:

  • Injury, death, or
  • Property damage over $1,000

Failure to file → license suspension

13 ADAS

ADAS: Advanced Driver Assistance Systems; an electronic system that helps the driver

Common ADAS features allowed in the driving test

  • Forward Collision Warning (FCW) – alerts you to possible front-end crashes
  • Automatic Emergency Braking (AEB) – brakes automatically if you don’t react
  • Lane Departure Warning / Lane Keeping Assist – warns or gently steers if you drift
  • Blind Spot Monitoring – alerts to vehicles in blind spots
  • Rear Cross Traffic Alert – warns when backing out of parking spots

These two are not allowed for driving test

  • Adaptive Cruise Control (ACC) – maintains speed and following distance
  • Parking Assist / Backup Cameras – help with parking and reversing

ADAS cannot replace the driver

  • They can fail in rain, snow, fog, glare, dirt-covered sensors, or poor lane markings
  • Sensors have blind spots and may misjudge speed/distance
  • It is your responsibility to always stay focused when driving
